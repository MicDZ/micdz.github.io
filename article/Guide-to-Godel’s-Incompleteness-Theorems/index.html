1.  Introduction

There are two interesting paradoxes that are closely related to Gödel’s incompleteness theorems: Russell’s paradox and Berry’s paradox.

Russell’s paradox:

Let R={x∣x∉x}\text{Let } R = \{ x | x \notin x \} Let R={x∣x∈/x}(1)

A more general form of Russell’s paradox is:

A barber only shaves those who do not shave themselves. Does the barber shave himself?

It’s easy to conduct:
The barber shaves himself ⇔\Leftrightarrow⇔ The barber does not shave himself.
If R∈RR \in RR∈R, then R∉RR \notin RR∈/R; If R∉RR \notin RR∈/R, then R∈RR \in RR∈R.
R∈R⇔R∉RR \in R \Leftrightarrow R \notin RR∈R⇔R∈/R leads to a contradiction.

Berry’s paradox:

All positive integers is definable in under sixty letters.

Let nnn be the smallest positive integer not definable in under sixty letters. Then nnn is definable in under sixty letters, leading to a contradiction.

These two paradoxes include self-reference, which is the key to Gödel’s incompleteness theorems.

2.  Hilbert’s Program

The main goal of Hilbert’s program was to lay down some basic axioms and then to write the axioms in a formal language that could be used to prove all mathematical theorems.

Hilbert wanted to prove this system have the following wonderful properties:

  1. Consistency: The system should not be able to prove a contradiction.
  2. Completeness: The system should be able to prove all true mathematical statements.
  3. Decidability: The system should be able to determine whether a statement is true or false.
  4. Conservativity: The system should be able to prove all true mathematical statements without adding any new axioms.

If Hilbert’s program could be completed, then all mathematical problems could be solved by a computer and math became impeccable.

Wir müssen wissen, wir werden wissen. (We must know, we will know.)
— David Hilbert

3.  Gödel’s First Incompleteness Theorem

Gödel’s first incompleteness theorem states that there exist true mathematical statements that cannot be proven in the system.

The proof of Gödel’s first incompleteness theorem is based on a self-reference paradox.

Here’s a simplified, informal rundown of how Gödel proved his theorems.

3.1.  Gödel Numbering

Gödel numbering is a way to encode mathematical statements as numbers.

  1. Assign a unique number to each symbol.
    The whole symbol table is:
    Constant Sign Gödel Number Usual Meaning
    \~{} 1 Not
    ∨\lor∨ 2 Or
    ⊃\supset⊃ 3 If … then …
    ∃\exists∃ 4 There is an …
    === 5 Equals
    000 6 Zero
    SSS 7 Successor of
    ((( 8 Left parenthesis
    ))) 9 Right parenthesis
    ,,, 10 Comma
    +++ 11 Plus
    ×\times× 12 Times
  2. Assign prime numbers greater than 12 to the letters representing variables.
    For x,y,z,…x, y, z, \ldotsx,y,z,…, assign 13, 17, 19, …\ldots….

Then any combination of these symbols and variables — that is, any arithmetical formula or sequence of formulas that can be constructed — gets its own Gödel number.

To map a formula to a number, we can use the following formula:

Go¨del number=2a1×3a2×5a3×…×pnan\text{Gödel number} = 2^{a_1} \times 3^{a_2} \times 5^{a_3} \times \ldots \times p_n^{a_n} Go¨del number=2a1​×3a2​×5a3​×…×pnan​​(2)

where a1,a2,…,ana_1, a_2, \ldots, a_na1​,a2​,…,an​ are the Gödel numbers of the symbols in the formula.

Take the formula x×0=0x \times 0 = 0x×0=0 as a simple example:

The Gödel formula of x×0=0x \times 0 = 0x×0=0 is 213×56×1211×66×562^{13} \times 5^{6} \times 12^{11} \times 6^{6} \times 5^{6}213×56×1211×66×56.

This construct method ensure that all the formulas in the system can be encoded to the only number with a multiplication of a bunch of prime numbers.

Since all the number can be uniquely factorized into prime numbers, we can decode the number to the only original formula easily by factorizing the Gödel number.

3.2.  Arithmetizing Metamathematics

The real boon is that even statements about arithmetic formulas, called metamathematical statements, can themselves be translated into formulas with Gödel numbers of their own.

For example, the statement “The first symbol of the formula ~(0 = 0) is a tilde.” This (true) metamathematical statement about ~(0 = 0) translates into a statement about the formula’s Gödel number — namely, that its first exponent is 1, the Gödel number for a tilde. In other words, our statement says that 21×38×56×75×116×1392^1 \times 38 \times 56 \times 75 \times 116 \times 13921×38×56×75×116×139 has only a single factor of 2. Had ~(0 = 0) begun with any symbol other than a tilde, its Gödel number would have at least two factors of 222. So, more precisely, 222 is a factor of 21×38×56×75×116×1392^1 \times 38 \times 56 \times 75 \times 116 \times 13921×38×56×75×116×139, but 222^222 is not a factor. Through this kind of mapping, we can translate metamathematical statements into statements about Gödel numbers.

Conversion into symbols is also possible for the metamathematical statement, “There exists some sequence of formulas with Gödel number x that proves the formula with Gödel number k” — or, in short, “The formula with Gödel number k can be proved.” The ability to “arithmetize” this kind of statement set the stage for the coup.

It’s noticable that the way of mapping is not the key of Gödel’s incompleteness theorems, but it is the foundation of the proof. Utlizing any other mapping method, the proof can still be conducted. We don’t need to care about whether all the math statements can be encoded to numbers or not, for now.

3.3.  The Proof

3.3.1.  Take, Find and Replace

The whole proof of Gödel’s first incompleteness theorem is based on a self-reference paradox. Self is what makes all the magic happen.

To make it easier to understand I construct a statement MMM that says, (∃x)(x=sy)(\exists x)(x=sy)(∃x)(x=sy). The Gödel number of MMM is marked as mmm.

Then we replace yyy in MMM with mmm and get a new statement NNN that says, (∃x)(x=sm)(\exists x)(x=sm)(∃x)(x=sm). The Gödel number of NNN is marked as nnn.

Now we can define a special function called sub(a,b,c)\mathrm{sub}(a, b, c)sub(a,b,c). The result of sub(a,b,c)\mathrm{sub}(a, b, c)sub(a,b,c) is a Gödel number of a new proposition.

The construction of the new proposition of sub(a,b,c)\mathrm{sub}(a, b, c)sub(a,b,c) contains three steps:

  1. Take the proposition with Gödel number aaa marked as AAA.
  2. Find the position of the symbol with Gödel number ccc in the proposition AAA.
  3. Replace the position with the number bbb.

Let’s continue to use the same example as above, we want to find what is the proposition of Gödel number sub(m,m,17)\mathrm{sub}(m, m, 17)sub(m,m,17).

We follow the TFR three steps:

  1. Take the proposition with Gödel number mmm.
    The proposition is (∃x)(x=sy)(\exists x)(x=sy)(∃x)(x=sy) marked as MMM.
  2. Find the position of the symbol with Gödel number 171717 in the proposition.
    The symbol with Gödel number 171717 is yyy.
  3. Replace the position with the number mmm.
    The proposition becomes (∃x)(x=sm)(\exists x)(x=sm)(∃x)(x=sm).

3.3.2.  The Construction of a Self-Reference Proposition

Firstly, we construct a new proposition that says, “it is impossible to prove the proposition with Gödel number sub(y,y,17)\mathrm{sub}(y, y, 17)sub(y,y,17)”. We mark the proposition as NNN with Gödel number nnn.

Here may casued some confusion. We still use the TFR three steps to find the proposition of Gödel number sub(y,y,17)\mathrm{sub}(y, y, 17)sub(y,y,17):

  1. Take the proposition with Gödel number yyy.
    The proposition is marked as YYY. The proposition may contains a lots of symbols, including yyy which is the symbol with Gödel number 171717.
  2. Find the position of the symbol with Gödel number 171717 which is yyy in the proposition YYY.
  3. Replace the position with the number yyy.

The confusing part is that yyy sometimes acts as a symbol and sometimes acts as a number. In step 2, we need to find the position of the symbol with Gödel number 171717 in the proposition YYY. But in step 3, we need to replace the position with the number yyy from the original sub(y,y,17)\mathrm{sub}(y,y,17)sub(y,y,17).

The symbol yyy here appear to enact different roles, but they are ensentially the same.

Previously, we have a proposition NNN that says, “it is impossible to prove the proposition with Gödel number sub(y,y,17)\mathrm{sub}(y, y, 17)sub(y,y,17)”. The Gödel number of NNN is nnn.

We continue our construction and get a new proposition PPP that says, “it is impossible to prove the proposition with Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17)”.

Focusing on the Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17) in PPP, we still use the TFR three steps to find the Gödel number of the Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17):

  1. Take the proposition with Gödel number nnn.
    The proposition is NNN which says “it is impossible to prove the proposition with Gödel number sub(y,y,17)\mathrm{sub}(y, y, 17)sub(y,y,17)”.
  2. Find the position of the symbol with Gödel number 171717 in the proposition.
  3. Replace the position with the number nnn.

Finally, we get the proposition of Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17) that says, “it is impossible to prove the proposition with Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17)”. The Gödel number of this proposition is coincidentally sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17).

Let’s take a careful look at Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17) and it’s proposition PPP. The proposition says, “it is impossible to prove the proposition with Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17)”.

If PPP is false, which means it is possible to prove the proposition with Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17), then the proposition with Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17) is true. But the proposition with Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17) says, “it is impossible to prove the proposition with Gödel number sub(n,n,17)\mathrm{sub}(n, n, 17)sub(n,n,17)”. This leads to a contradiction.

There should be one and only one way to fix the paradox: the proposition PPP is true.

Thus proposition PPP becomes a true but unprovable proposition, which proves Hilbert’s completeness property is impossible.

This is the core of Gödel’s first incompleteness theorem.

Any consistent formal system FFF within which a certain amount of elementary arithmetic can be carried out is incomplete; i.e., there are statements of the language of FFF which can neither be proved nor disproved in FFF.

4.  Gödel’s Second Incompleteness Theorem

For any consistent formal system FFF within which a certain amount of elementary arithmetic can be carried out, the consistency of FFF cannot be proved in FFF itself.

The only condition we set to prove the first incompleteness theorem is the consistency of the system.

The second incompleteness theorem is a direct result of the first incompleteness theorem. If the consistency of FFF can be proved in FFF, then we can prove the proposition PPP in FFF. But the proposition PPP is unprovable in FFF. This leads to a contradiction.

The one and only way to fix the paradox is that the consistency of FFF cannot be proved in FFF itself.

As a side note, Gödel immigrated to the United States in 1947. When he took the citizenship test, he found a contradiction in the U.S. Constitution and told to Albert Einstein. However, he didn’t point it out to others.

5.  Turing’s Halting Problem

Gödel’s incompleteness theorems pointed out the consistency and completeness of a formal system cannot be proved in the system itself. Turing’s halting problem further proved that the mathematics is undecidable.
